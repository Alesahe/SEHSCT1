Calendar

an outline of astronomical events in 2025

On 3 January, the waxing-crescent moon and Venus will be visible 3.26° apart to the West; in conjunction.


The Quadrantids meteor shower will reach its maximum activity on 3 to 4 January. One of the most reliable showers annually, the Quadrantids will reach a zenithal hourly rate (ZHR) of 110 meteors an hour under ideal conditions. Another interesting fact about this particular shower is that the meteors originate from the asteroid 2003 EH₁, rather than a comet like most other showers.


On 4 January, the waxing-crescent moon and Saturn will be visible 3.59° apart to the West; in conjunction.


On 5 January, the Earth will reach perihelion; in its elliptical orbit, it will move closer to the Sun than at any other point during the year. Therefore, the Sun will be 7% brighter than the Earth’s farthest point, or aphelion! On this day, the celestial bodies will only be 147, 104, 000km apart.


On 5 January, Saturn will be occulted by the moon; that is, the moon will pass in front of the planet.


On 6 January, Neptune will be occulted by the moon; that is, the moon will pass in front of the planet.


On 8 January, the moon will be in perigee; in its elliptical orbit, it will move closer to Earth than at any other point during its cycle. Therefore, it will appear 14% bigger than at its farthest point, or apogee!


On 10 January, Venus will be at its Eastern elongation, where it is the closest to Earth than at any other point during the celestial bodies’ elliptical orbits.


On 12 January, exactly half of the planet Venus will appear illuminated by the Sun.


On 13 January, the asteroid 887 Alinda will be at opposition - it will reach the closest point to Earth in its orbit.


On 13 Jan, the comet C/2024 G3, otherwise known as ATLAS, will pass its perigee - meaning it is at its closest to Earth during its orbit!


The moon will be full on 13 January.


On 14 January, the waxing-crescent moon and Mars will be visible 4.3° apart to the North-East; in conjunction.


On 16 January, Mars will be at opposition - it will reach the closest point to Earth in its orbit.


On 17 January, the waning-gibbous moon and Mars will be visible 2.22° apart to the North-West; in conjunction.


On 18 January, Venus and Saturn will be visible 2.19° apart to the West; in conjunction.


The γ-Ursae Minorid meteor shower will reach its maximum activity on 19 January. One of the less reliable showers, the Minorids will reach a zenithal hourly rate (ZHR) of 3 meteors an hour under ideal conditions.


On 23 January, the star Pollux of the constellation Gemini and Mars will be visible 2.36° apart to the North-West; in conjunction.


On 25 January, the waning-crescent moon and the star Antares of the constellation Scorpius will be visible 2.26° apart to the East; in conjunction.


The moon will be new on 29 January.




On 1 February, the waxing-crescent moon and Saturn will be visible 3.06° apart to the West; in conjunction.


On 10 February, the waxing-gibbous moon and Mars will be visible 3.22° apart to the North-West; in conjunction.


The moon will be full on 12 February.


On 13 February, the asteroid 29 Amphitrite will be at opposition - it will reach the closest point to Earth in its orbit.


On 17 February, the waning-gibbous moon and the star Spica of Virgo will be visible only 0.24° apart to the East; in conjunction.


On 18 February, the moon will be in apogee; in its elliptical orbit, it will move further from Earth than at any other point during its cycle. Therefore, it will appear 11% smaller than at its closest point, or perigee!
